Types of Stocks

Stocks can be classified into multiple categories on various parameters – size of the company, dividend payment, industry, risk, volatility, as well as fundamentals.

Stocks on the basis of ownership rules

  • Common stocks

    1. Common stock is a security that represents ownership in a corporation
    2. In a liquidation, common stockholders receive whatever assets remain after creditors, bondholders, and preferred stockholders are paid
    3. There are different varieties of stocks traded in the market. For example, value stocks are stocks that are lower in price in relation to their fundamentals. Growth stocks are companies that tend to increase in value due to growing earnings.
    4. Investors should diversify their portfolio by putting money into different securities based on their appetite for risk.

  • Preferred stocks

    1. Preference shares (preferred stock) are company stock with dividends that are paid to shareholders before common stock dividends are paid out.
    2. There are four types of preferred stock - cumulative (guaranteed), non-cumulative, participating and convertible.
    3. Preference shares are ideal for risk-averse investors and they are callable (the issuer can redeem them at any time).

    Stocks on the basis of market capitalisation

  • Small-cap stocks

    1. A small cap is generally a company with a market capitalisation of between $300 million and $2 billion.
    2. The advantage of investing in small cap stocks is the opportunity to beat institutional investors through growth opportunities.
    3. Small cap stocks have historically outperformed large cap stocks but have also been more volatile and riskier investments.

  • Mid-cap stocks

    1. Mid-cap is the term given to companies with a market cap (capitalisation)—or market value—between $2 billion and $10 billion.
    2. For companies, some of the appealing features of mid-cap companies are that they are expected to grow and increase profits, market share. and productivity; they are in the middle of their growth curve.
    3. Mid-cap stocks are useful in portfolio diversification because they provide a balance of growth and stability.

  • Large-cap stocks

    1. Large cap (sometimes called "big cap") refers to a company with a market capitalisation value of more than $10 billion.
    2. Large cap is a shortened version of the term "large market capitalisation."
    3. Market capitalization is calculated by multiplying the number of a company's shares outstanding by its stock price per share.

    Stocks on the basis of dividend payments

  • Income stocks

    1. Income stocks are stocks that offer regular and steady income, usually in the form of dividends, over a period of time with low exposure to risk.
    2. Most income stocks have lower levels of volatility than the overall stock market, and offer higher-than-market dividend yields.
    3. Ideal income stocks would also show a history of increasing dividends on a regular basis so as to keep up with inflation, which eats away at future cash payments.

  • Growth stocks

    1. A growth stock is any share in a company that is anticipated to grow at a rate significantly above the average growth for the market.
    2. These stocks generally do not pay dividends.
    3. This is because the issuers of growth stocks are usually companies that want to reinvest any earnings they accrue in order to accelerate growth in the short term.
    4. When investors invest in growth stocks, they anticipate that they will earn money through capital gains when they eventually sell their shares in the future.
    5. They may not have earnings at the present moment but are expected to in the future.

    Stocks on the basis of risk

  • Blue chip stocks

    1. A blue-chip stock is a huge company with an excellent reputation.
    2. These are typically large, well-established and financially sound companies that have operated for many years and that have dependable earnings, often paying dividends to investors.
    3. A blue-chip stock typically has a market capitalization in the billions, is generally the market leader or among the top three companies in its sector, and is more often than not a household name.
    4. For all of these reasons, blue-chip stocks are among the most popular to buy among investors. Some examples of blue-chip stocks are IBM Corp., Coca-Cola Co. and Boeing Co.

  • Penny stocks

    1. A penny stock refers to a small company's stock that typically trades for less than $5 per share.
    2. Penny stocks are most suitable for investors who have a high tolerance for risk
    3. Although some penny stocks trade on large exchanges such as the NYSE, most penny stocks trade via over the counter through the OTC Bulletin Board (OTCBB).
    4. While there can be sizeable gains in trading penny stocks, there are also equal risks of losing a significant amount of an investment in a short period.

    Stocks on the basis of price trends

  • Cyclical stocks

    1. Cyclical stocks are affected by macroeconomic changes, where its returns follow the cycles of an economy.
    2. Cyclical stocks usually have higher volatility and are expected to produce higher returns during periods of economic strength.
    3. Cyclical stocks are the opposite of defensive stocks. Cyclical stocks include discretionary companies, such as Starbucks or Nike.

  • Defensive stocks

    1. A defensive stock is a stock that provides consistent dividends and stable earnings regardless of the state of the overall stock market.
    2. Well-established companies, such as Procter & Gamble, Johnson & Johnson, Philip Morris International, and Coca-Cola, are considered defensive stocks.
    3. Defensive stocks offer the substantial benefit of similar long-term gains with lower risk than other stocks.
    4. On the downside, the low volatility of defensive stocks often leads to smaller gains during bull markets and a cycle of mistiming the market.
